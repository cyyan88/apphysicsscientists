Historical Scientists

Historical

Austria

Schrödinger’s Wave Equation, which gives the position of a particle (electron) at a certain position, was his most important contribution. This is important because the equation was the first to effectively describe the behavior of an electron and can be applied to both physics and chemistry, such as in creating new chemicals and medicines.

Schrödinger started seriously researching in 1920, which was post World War I, after serving as an officer. He also researched through WWII, where he escaped to Italy before returning to Italy later in his life.

Denmark

Bohr’s most important discovery was the liquid droplet theory, which said that neutrons and protons behaved like drops of liquid in the nuclei. This was important because it allowed Hahn and Strassmann to later create the atomic bomb, and future scientists to begin research into nuclear physics.

Bohr’s research was done after Denmark’s industrial revolution, and partially during World War I. Similarly to Schrödinger, he left Denmark for Sweden then England during WWII, before returning to Denmark.

England

Dalton’s Atomic Theory, which said that all matter was made of the smallest unit atoms, and that the same type of atom had the same mass and properties. This is because knowledge of atoms at this level is the foundation of chemistry, and allowed future scientists to better explore relationships between atoms and conceptualize how atoms worked to create new materials.

Dalton’s research corresponded with England’s Industrial Revolution, and wars including the French Revolutionary Wars and Napoleonic Wars.

United Kingdom

Thomson’s most significant contribution was his cathode ray experiment, which resulted in the discovery of the electron, and the knowledge that they were negatively charged. This is significant because it allowed for additional research using electrons, which led to a greater understanding of electricity and energy.

Thomson’s most significant research was done during the late 1800s, which was the period of Victorian England. This period included industrialization, a growing economy, and a more stratified class system.

Poland

Curie’s most significant contribution is the discovery of Radium and Polonium. Both of these atoms have practical uses, such as in medicine and space travel. Moreover, the discoveries gave scientists a better understanding of nuclear physics and do further research into nuclear energy and other current applications.

Curie did some of her research during World War I, which was useful because it allowed her to promote the use of portable X-rays, which were based on radioactivity.

New Zealand and England

Rutherford’s most important contribution was the discovery of radioactivity and alpha and beta radiation. This is important because radiation is used today in medicine, and knowledge that radiation exists highlights the danger of nuclear disasters and certain chemicals.

Rutherford’s research was done during the Industrial Revolution and World War I. He also worked with scientists such as Thomson and Bohr during this time period.

Modern

Stanford University, US

Susskind did research in string theory, and won the J.J. Sakurai Prize for his work. He also was on the team that discovered the Veneziano Dual Resonance Model and was the first person to propose a string theory landscape.

Susskind’s work is important because he is considered the father of string theory, a field that has practical applications in explaining space and time. He laid the foundation for new physics models that explain the existence of different dimensions.

University of Chicago, US

Nambu did research in quantum chromodynamics, and proposed a color charge, which explains how different quarks interact. He also contributed to string theory by relating it to the dual resonance model.

Nambu’s work is important because he was considered another founder of string theory, which has applications in understanding how phenomena like black holes work and increases our understanding of the universe.

MIT, US

Drexler pioneered the field of nanotechnology, inspired by his work with NASA solar sails. He also came up with the idea of self-replicating nanotechnology and gray goo, where if out of control, self-replicators would use up all of the Earth’s biomass in replicating.

Drexler’s work is important because he was a founder of nanotech, which has applications in fields like manufacturing, food, medicine, and energy. This is useful because nanotech is generally more efficient and precise.

University College London, UK

Seaton researched electron collisions with atoms and ions, and impacts, and radiative accelerations, which is a phenomenon found in stars. Outside of atomic physics, he also contributed to quantum defect theory.

Seaton’s work is important because he came up with various models and formulas describing atomic/electron/ion collisions that can be applied in future research and increases our understanding of the atom.

Department of Energy's National Nuclear Security Administration, US

Frazier does research with nuclear weapons stockpiling with the Nuclear Department of Energy. She also did work on the nuclear shell model in terms of nuclear testing, and plasma science.

Frazier’s work is important because it ensures that the US’s nuclear weapon stockpile is safe, and doesn’t require additional nuclear testing which could be dangerous and harmful to the environment. It sets a safer precedent for nuclear technology going forward.

Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory, US

Hartouni does work in high energy physics and accelerators. At LLNL, he worked on a relativistic heavy ion collider and particle/neutrino injectors. He also studies quark production and Au + Au collisions.

Hartouni’s work is important because it can be applied to the US’s national security against nuclear weapons. His work on radiology, injectors, and accelerators will also result in future experiments being done more easily and effectively.
